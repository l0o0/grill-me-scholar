# Research Lenses

Read only after the user's field is known. Use the smallest relevant set of checks. These are prompts for reasoning, not a questionnaire; ask only the single question that resolves the current highest-impact uncertainty.

## Humanities

- Identify the primary texts, artifacts, archives, events, or practices.
- Bound the corpus by place, period, language, genre, creator, or archive.
- Distinguish interpretation, comparison, genealogy, critique, and historical explanation.
- Test whether the interpretive framework illuminates the material rather than predetermining the conclusion.
- Check source access, provenance, translation, archival silences, and positionality.

## Social Sciences

- Define the population, setting, period, phenomenon, and unit of analysis.
- Separate concepts, observable indicators, mechanisms, outcomes, and rival explanations.
- Distinguish descriptive, interpretive, causal, comparative, and evaluative questions.
- Check sampling, case selection, data access, measurement validity, and inference limits.
- Check consent, privacy, participant risk, and applicable ethics review.
- Do not imply causality when the feasible design supports only association or interpretation.

## Science and Engineering

- Define the system, boundary conditions, inputs, outputs, and measurable variables.
- State the hypothesis, model, design objective, or performance claim that could be tested.
- Check instruments, baselines, controls, uncertainty, sample size, and reproducibility.
- Distinguish proof of concept, optimization, benchmarking, mechanism testing, and deployment evaluation.
- Check equipment, compute, materials, safety, and access constraints before confirming scope.

## Medicine and Health

- Define population, condition, intervention or exposure, comparison, outcome, and time horizon when applicable.
- Distinguish clinical, epidemiological, diagnostic, implementation, and health-services questions.
- Check clinical importance separately from statistical detectability.
- Check design fit, selection bias, confounding, measurement validity, and inference limits.
- Check recruitment, records or specimen access, consent, privacy, risk, and ethics review.
- Never offer a topic as feasible if required participants or protected data are inaccessible.

## Design and Practice Research

- Define the setting, stakeholders, unmet need, artifact or intervention, and intended change.
- Distinguish research through design, design research, action research, and program evaluation.
- Make the evaluation criteria and evidence of learning explicit.
- Check stakeholder participation, iteration time, deployment access, and researcher influence.
- Ensure the contribution is knowledge gained, not merely the production of an artifact.

## Interdisciplinary Topics

For interdisciplinary topics, identify how each field frames the question and whether it contributes theory, method, evidence, or context. Name a primary field only when doing so clarifies responsibility; allow co-owned or transdisciplinary framing when it does not. Combine checks only where they affect validity or feasibility. If the fields make incompatible assumptions about evidence, surface that conflict before discussing methods.
